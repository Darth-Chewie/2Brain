My focus is on PKM for research (that's what I teach) so please read my comment with that in mind.

First, let me talk a bit about "atomic notes". The person who came up with the concept of atomicity never said atomic notes need to be small. Instead, he talked about "separation of concerns".

Separation of concerns means that each note should be concerned about a different thing.

In practice, when you have one note per paper you read, that is already a "separation of concerns". But for research purposes that is often not useful. Instead, we want our notes to represent ideas.

Why?

Let us think about "when" we need to use our notes (for research):

-   for provenance, i.e. to say where an idea came from, or
    
-   to strengthen our argument, by providing references to other sources (via an argumentation move called "backing")
    

That means, we have the idea already and we need to find the source where we encountered that idea (quickly!).

If our notes are idea-based notes then we just open that note for the idea and find all sources where the idea appears. Done.

If our notes are document-based notes (like the ones you mentioned) then you need to remember in which papers the idea appears (to find the note) or try your luck with a keyword or tag-based search. Much harder than having the idea note directly.

Also, idea-based notes help in growing our knowledge and research contribution.

We read many papers not to have a summary for the sake of a summary. Instead, we want to synthesise the literature based on a group of ideas (our conceptual framework) so, ideally, we want one note mentioning all sources where that idea appears and also mentioning how that idea is relevant to our work. THAT is the synthesis already (even if in draft mode).

To complete, each paper we write is an argument. Arguments are basically chains of ideas in a "premises lead to conclusion" format. Well, what are premises and conclusions if not ideas themselves?

When taking idea-notes, building an argument becomes like playing Lego and it also becomes easier to find which pieces are missing to complete our argument. Even more, we can reuse a piece (idea) in multiple argument chains.

So.. that's why I think taking idea-based notes (atomic notes) is important for research. As soon as you abandon this concept that "atomic notes are small".

Btw, if you use atomic notes you may also want to change into idea-based reference management rather than the current document-based reference management promoted by Zotero and other apps.

I hope this reflection helps 🙏
